About maps of residuals from regression

Aggregate measures at the country level are often strongly influenced by per capita income. The graph below illustrates a typical relationship, that between per capita income and the maternal mortality rate.

{% load static %}

Maternal mortality rate falls sharply with increasing per capita income. Each country is represented by a black dot; the curved blue line indicates an estimate of the relationship between the two variables (estimation is done by a technique known as locally weighted scatterplot smoothing). Note the use of scientific notation; the value 5e+04 is a five with four trailing zeroes, or 50,000.

The relationship is close but not exact. The maps of residuals from this regression indicate how much each country's value of the given indicator deviates from what would be expected, given its per-capita income. For example, consider Mexico and the United States. Hovering over the map at top shows that Mexico's maternal mortality rate is 38 per 100,000 live births, while the corresponding figure for the United States is 14 (you can magnify the image by "pulling" the map closer using mouse, fingers, or touchpad).

The map at bottom displays residuals from a regression using GDP per capita to explain variance in maternal mortality. Hovering again over the two countries indicates that Mexico's maternal mortality rate is about 15 per 100,000 below what is expected given its GDP per capita, while the US figure is about 8 per 100,000 above what would be predicted given its per-capita income.

Norway's maternal mortality rate, at 5 per 100,000, is near the minimum across all countries. Looking at the map of residuals shows that this value is nearly identical to the value predicted by its per-capita GDP (the residual is about -0.44).

Interpretation of the maps of residuals is not completely straightforward. As a rough approximation, the residuals indicate by how much a country is "over-performing" or "under-performing" on the given indicator. Mexico performs relatively well, with fewer maternal deaths than its per-capita income would predict. The United States under-performs, with more maternal deaths than a typical country with its GDP per capita is expected to have. Norway performs at an average level for its (high) per capita income.
